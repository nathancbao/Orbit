# Milestone 3 — User Experience Summary

## Overview

We had 10 users test Orbit and conducted post-usage interviews with all of them, focusing on Pain, Promise, Anxiety, and Inertia. Users ranged from freshmen to PhD researchers, representing a variety of social needs and tech comfort levels. Testing was conducted over single-day sessions of 2–5 hours each, as the app's community-dependent nature made multi-day sustained usage impractical without a real user base.

All 10 users tested the app for 1 day. No user achieved multi-day sustained usage, primarily because the community was too small to generate organic activity between sessions.

---

## Pain

Every user validated the core pain point — **it is hard to casually meet new people on a college campus after the initial freshman welcome period.**

- **Coordination friction is the immediate pain:** Even when users have people to hang out with, getting everyone to agree on plans is slow and frustrating. Casey described this vividly — organizing hangouts through Discord required herding multiple people through indecision, and the back-and-forth was the worst part. Ben had a similar experience trying to set up a pickup basketball game and getting no responses.
- **Niche interest isolation:** Several users have hobbies their current friend group doesn't share. Nishad's experience was particularly telling — he's into boxing and jiu jitsu, but none of his friends are. His only option was cold-approaching strangers at the gym, which worked but was awkward and unreliable. He wouldn't use Orbit for regular hangouts but sees clear value for connecting with people around niche activities.
- **No middle ground between formal clubs and nothing:** Campus orgs require full commitment — dues, meetings, schedules. Without them, there's no structure for casual group activities (Adam, Thomas). Nathan echoed this, noting that alternatives like Discord and Facebook exist but lack a mechanism for facilitating real-life meetups specifically.
- **Social bubble stagnation:** Friends are all from the same major or dorm; activities repeat (Audrey, Claire, Rachie).
- **Surface-level freshman connections:** Jay described the gap between knowing someone's name and actually doing stuff together — the hardest part of freshman social life that nobody warns you about.

---

## Promise

Users saw Orbit's value proposition clearly — **a low-pressure, casual way to find and do activities with like-minded people.**

- **Pods as icebreakers:** The pod structure was the most validated feature across all interviews. Casey noted that pods organize groups and suggest activities, reducing coordination overhead. Nishad said pods break the ice automatically — they skip the awkward cold-approach phase and give shy people a structured way to form groups. Nathan specifically highlighted that pods let you skip the "introduce yourself" phase and get straight to the activity. Audrey, Jay, and others reinforced this.
- **Orbit fills a planning gap that cold approaches can't:** Nishad articulated this best — you can cold-approach someone for a spontaneous gym session, but you'd never approach a stranger to invite them to a weekend BBQ. There's no good way to find people for planned group events unless they're already your friends. Orbit fills that specific gap.
- **The casual tone resonated.** Audrey noted it doesn't feel like a dating app, LinkedIn, or formal org. The low-commitment framing made Jay comfortable joining pods without overthinking it.
- **AI suggestions created discovery moments** for some users — Jay's photography recommendation and Audrey's live music pod were things they wouldn't have searched for but were genuinely interested in.
- **Flex missions worked for the right users.** Jay completed one on day one. Audrey saved one. Rachie figured it out after one use.

---

## Anxiety

The main friction points were around **clarity and trust**, not around the concept itself:

- **Confusing terminology:** "Mission," "pod," and "flex mission" were unclear to first-time users. Four users (Claire, Rachie, Ben, Thomas) didn't fully understand what these terms meant. Claire's experience was the most impactful — the confusion blocked her from engaging with AI suggestions and flex missions entirely. She didn't reject those features; she just never reached them. Thomas suggested a tutorial, as did Nishad's discussion around new feature adoption.
- **AI trust gap:** Casey's feedback was representative of the broader pattern — he found suggestions interesting but slightly generic, and was hesitant to trust them since the methods were unclear. The match percentage confused Ben. The consensus across users: AI helps but needs more profile data and transparency to be consistently useful. Notably, most users (Nishad, Nathan, Audrey, Jay) saw the AI as low-risk — worst case, you just browse manually.
- **Fear of going first:** Audrey explicitly said she doesn't want to be the first to suggest something in a pod of strangers. Jay is willing to initiate but needs reciprocation — silence signals a dead app to him.
- **People filtering concern:** Thomas raised that pods have strong activity filtering but little people filtering, which could attract incompatible individuals.

---

## Inertia

The biggest barrier to adoption is the **cold start problem** — and most users named it directly.

- **The chicken-and-egg problem:** Nishad articulated this most clearly — the app's value depends on having users, but attracting users requires the app to already have value. Early users open the app expecting it to work, find no content or active users, and uninstall. He recommended focusing on a single niche community and building outward, which aligns with Casey's observation that not enough people are on Orbit yet and Nathan's note that the user base is effectively nonexistent.
- **Existing alternatives are low-friction:** Instagram DMs, Discord servers, and texting friends require zero learning curve and already have network density (Adam, Casey, Thomas). Casey specifically compared Discord's unlimited server sizes to Orbit's deliberately small pods — more selection in Discord, but Orbit is better for initiating real-life meetups.
- **Established social lives reduce urgency:** Casey, Nathan, Claire, and Rachie all have functional friend groups. For them, Orbit is a "nice to have" — they see the value but don't feel enough pain to actively change their behavior. Casey acknowledged he may not be the target audience given his solid social life and busy PhD schedule.
- **Empty pods kill momentum:** Users join pods but find no activity, no plans, and no responses. Nathan found unresponsive or test accounts when he tried joining pods. Without reciprocation, even willing initiators like Jay will disengage.
- **The old way has real advantages:** Nathan noted that online friendships are quicker and more instantaneous. Nishad pointed out that cold approaches build social skills and give immediate answers. But both acknowledged Orbit's advantages — .edu email verification for safety (Nathan) and the ability to plan group events with strangers (Nishad).

---

## Key Takeaways & Recommendations

1. **The core problem is validated.** All 10 users agreed that casual social connection on campus is broken. The pod concept resonated as a natural icebreaker that lowers the barrier to meeting people. Nishad and Casey both highlighted the coordination and awkwardness of the alternatives.

2. **The cold start problem is the #1 existential risk.** Every user flagged low user density as the main reason they might not return. Nishad's suggestion to focus on a single niche and build outward is the most actionable path forward. **Recommendation:** Saturate one specific community (a dorm, a club, an interest group) before expanding.

3. **Pods are the strongest feature — but they need activation.** The pod concept was validated by nearly every user, from Casey and Nathan appreciating the group-forming structure to Jay completing a flex mission on day one. The problem is that pods go silent after creation. **Recommendation:** Auto-generate icebreaker prompts or suggested plans in new pods to break the initial silence.

4. **Terminology needs simplification.** "Mission," "pod," and "flex" are not self-explanatory. Four users were confused enough that it blocked engagement with features. Claire's entire session was hampered by it. **Recommendation:** Add a brief onboarding walkthrough on first login, or rename features using plainer language.

5. **AI suggestions are promising but need more data.** Casey's observation that suggestions are generic due to limited profile data was echoed by multiple users. Accuracy was inconsistent, though users like Jay saw a 2-out-of-3 hit rate. **Recommendation:** Add 2–3 onboarding preference questions to improve recommendation quality immediately.

6. **Flex missions work for the right users.** Jay (freshman, exploratory mindset) completed one on day one. Planners like Adam and Casey avoided it entirely. Nishad saw value in planned group events over spontaneous ones. **Recommendation:** Frame flex missions with more detail so planners feel comfortable, while keeping the spontaneous appeal for explorers.

7. **Strongest early adopter profile:** Freshmen with free time, open to spontaneity, and actively seeking social connections (Jay is the clearest example). Users with niche hobbies their friends don't share are a strong secondary segment (Nishad). These users should be the primary acquisition targets.
