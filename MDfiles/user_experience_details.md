## Interview 1: Casey Felton (PhD Candidate, Linguistics TA)

**Session length:** ~3 hours (1 day)

---

### Phase 2: The Task (The Core Loop)
**Scenario given:** "Imagine you are planning a trip for next weekend. Show me how you would use this tool to get a list of restaurants."

- Casey was able to plan a trip quite quickly and with no guidance and minimal confusion.
- This was much faster than the app was the previous week — a very positive sign.
- He did take a bit of time to figure out the voyage page, but that is fine and expected.

---

### Phase 3: The AI "Vibe Check"
**Questions asked:**
- "Look at the answer the AI just gave you."
- "Is this useful?"
- "Do you trust it?"
- "What would you do with this information next?"

- Casey thought that the AI suggestions were interesting.
- He thought they were pretty accurate (not 100%, but they are just suggestions and are never 100% accurate).
- He thinks they are a bit generic, since there is minimal information for them to work off of.
- He is hesitant to trust it since the methods it works with are slightly unclear.
- However, he says it's a good addition since it is just a suggestion and not forcing users to do anything.

---

### Qualitative User Feedback

- The app isn't currently widely used, so it is difficult to find help if you don't know a feature. This is a non-problem for him since he can ask Adrian or the Orbit team, but he thinks this may be a potential issue for users not connected to the Orbit team. He thinks a support page or troubleshooting tool might be helpful.
- He suggests potentially breaking down sports into different categories once the app gets bigger, since "sports" may be too broad. That is fine for now while there aren't many users.
- App looked clean. UI clean.
- Everything attempted worked as expected. He did not run into bugs and was able to play with events.

**Pain:** Casey did not really have trouble making friends and has good social groups. However, he tried Orbit in order to plan new events that he hasn't tried before. He hypothesizes that it would be good at solving that problem. However, there aren't really active users yet on the app so he said he can only hypothesize and not really know for certain.

- He did not really use the flex mission feature. When asked why, he said he prefers structured events since he has a very busy schedule (being a PhD researcher). Because of this, he did not use the spontaneous feature.
- The last time he made a spontaneous hangout was a month ago with friends — he does not do those often, and when he does, he would rather it be with friends rather than with unfamiliar people.
- He says the app may be solving a real problem for people who have trouble meeting new people. The app forces people into pods when they sign up for an activity, so it may serve as a good icebreaker for being social.
- He says he may not be the target audience, since he has a solid social life and a busy schedule.

### Post-Usage Script

**Pain (Push):** "Walk me through the last time you dealt with making or finding friends to do certain activities you love. What was the absolute worst part about it?"

> Casey said that he did this through Discord, by joining a server off of Reddit, talking to them, and planning a hangout. The worst part was coordination and having everyone agree on something, which took some time with multiple people.

**Promise (Pull):** "When you first opened our app, what specific outcome were you hoping it would magically do for you?"

> Casey hoped that there would be tools like meetup coordinators and stuff to help prevent the slowness of people making decisions and being indecisive. He said this was kind of present but the app did not have too much of this. However, it did sort together groups and suggest activities to find, which was cool.

**Anxiety (Friction):** "When you saw the AI's output, what made you hesitate? What were you worried it got wrong?"

> Casey thought that the AI suggestions were interesting. He thought they were pretty accurate (not 100%, but it's just a suggestion anyway, and they are never 100% accurate). He thinks they are a bit generic, since there is minimal information for them to work off of, and he is hesitant to trust it since the methods it works with are slightly unclear. However, he says it's a good addition since it's just a suggestion and not forcing users to do anything.

**Inertia (Status Quo):** "Walk me through the exact moment you decided to close the app and just do it your old way. Why was the old way easier in that second?"

> He did not really do this, but when he tried the old way through Discord, what made it easier was that there are vastly more people in Discord. Also, servers in Discord don't have people limits while pods do, so you have more people you can choose from and screen. The pods are useful for finding people in the first place, as screening is hit or miss in Discord. So he said the old way is easier in terms of options — this is NOT because Discord has way more users than the app, so don't confuse it with that. It is mainly because of the format of "Discord servers" vs the format of "Orbit pods," which were designed to be much smaller tight-knit groups. He does understand that Orbit does it this way in order to initiate real life meetups as soon as possible. Discord does not have this constraint.

## Interview 2: Nishad (CS New Grad)
**Interviewed by:** Adrian  
**Session length:** ~3–5 hours (1 day)

> Note: Could not dedicate 3 days of use since there were many bug fixes being smoothed out. Also, the app is community-based and there isn't a real community yet, so 3 days of use is not too feasible for this format.

---

### Qualitative User Feedback

**Principle 1: Know the data before you talk to them**

- Nishad said the UI was clean and wouldn't take too long to figure out. However, he is very tech savvy.
- He thinks any younger generation and tech-savvy older generation person can figure it out with minimal time, though it may take longer for less experienced people. He doubts less experienced people are the target audience anyway.
- He said the activity finder with AI suggestions was effective — the suggestions are pretty good and do take your interests into consideration.
- Nishad mentioned that a lot of the events do focus around a similar % of match. This is because there is not a ton of data that the AI currently feeds off of (just tags + event description), so the suggestion isn't too strong — but it is better than just browsing events. He thinks it's an improvement on the overall app.

**Understanding if we're solving a real problem:**

- He thinks the app could be solving a problem for people who find it difficult to make friends, and also for people who have friends but have interests their friends don't share.
- Nishad is interested in boxing and jiu jitsu, but most of his friends don't share those interests. He thinks he won't use Orbit much for regular hangouts (he would rather just go with his friend group), but he would potentially use it to find people for his niche hobbies, which his usual friends don't really want to do. He thinks this adds value to the app and that we should focus on this aspect going forward.
- Nishad brought up the **"cold start problem"** — a big problem for most social platforms. It is tough to attract users when the app's value depends on other users being there. Early users may open the app expecting events to work, but find no context, activities, or mutual users. They uninstall and you get no userbase. This creates a catch-22: the app's whole value relies on having many users, but it's hard to get many users when the value isn't high yet.
- He suggests dialing in on a niche (such as a school club focus) and growing from there, adding more niches once there's a cult following in the first niche.

---

### Post-Usage Script

**Pain (Push):** "Walk me through the last time you dealt with making or finding friends to do certain activities you love. What was the absolute worst part about it?"

> He said the last time was when he was trying to find people to do martial arts with. He did this by approaching and asking people who were on the heavy bags in the gym. He said the worst part is it was sort of awkward, but it did work well.

**Promise (Pull):** "When you first opened our app, what specific outcome were you hoping it would magically do for you?"

> He was hoping the app would suggest you to people in order to break the ice and skip the awkward stages. He says it does this well with the pods feature, which places you into pods. He can't speak for it too much since there wasn't actually a community, so the app wasn't truly usable. However, he says he sees the vision and thinks that pods for events could help shy people find what they are looking for (forming a group).

**Anxiety (Friction):** "When you saw the AI's output, what made you hesitate? What were you worried it got wrong?"

> He thought AI was an interesting addition. It mainly works for suggestions, so he said he didn't really hesitate — best case scenario, the suggestions would increase user experience, and worst case scenario, if the AI suggestions don't match, he can just manually work his way through the app, which he doesn't mind. He isn't sold on it but thinks it can only help.

**Inertia (Status Quo):** "Walk me through the exact moment you decided to close the app and just do it your old way. Why was the old way easier in that second?"

> He talked about doing it (meeting friends for boxing/jiu jitsu) the old way vs. the new way. He says the old way is still better in that it forces you to develop social skills and is immediate — you can get a quick yes or no. However, he thinks that Orbit and its pods are better in that you can find people to plan events with. With cold approaches, you can do spontaneous short events (like a gym session), but he says you wouldn't really plan a hiking trip or BBQ with people you don't know. (Imagine approaching someone and asking "wanna do a BBQ with 6 or 7 people this weekend?" — you're likely to get turned down.) There isn't really a way to get people for these kinds of planned events unless they are friends you already know. He says Orbit is very useful in that regard.

## Interview 3: Audrey
**Session length:** 1 day

---

### Qualitative User Feedback

**Q: What problem were you trying to solve?**

> "Most of my friends are from my major and we always end up doing the same things. I want to branch out and meet people I wouldn't normally cross paths with, but I don't know where to start."

She described feeling stuck in a social bubble where everyone knows each other and the activities repeat every weekend. She's interested in things like hiking, pottery, and live music but doesn't have anyone in her current circle who's into that. She's not looking to replace her friend group, just expand it in a low-pressure way.

**Q: First impressions after using it today?**

> "It felt welcoming honestly. I liked that it wasn't trying to be a dating app or a LinkedIn thing. It felt casual, like here are some people who want to do cool stuff, come join. I signed up and joined a weekend hiking pod and a thrift shopping one pretty fast."

The tone of the app clicked with her right away. She appreciated that it didn't feel transactional or overly structured. The casual framing made her more comfortable putting herself out there — comfort level is probably a bigger barrier for some users than usability.

**Q: What did you think of the AI suggestions?**

> "Some were really good. It suggested a live music pod which I loved. But then it also recommended a volleyball league and I'm the least athletic person alive. I think if it asked me a couple more questions upfront it could nail it better."

Similar to other users, the AI got some things right and some things wrong. She specifically called out wanting a better onboarding questionnaire, which could be a quick win for improving recommendation quality.

**Q: Did you try the flex mission feature?**

> "I actually did! There was one for trying a new coffee shop near campus and I thought why not. It seemed cool but the users were unresponsive. Still, I like that it gives you a reason to just go do something instead of sitting around debating what to do in the group chat."

She was more open to flex missions than some other users, though she still hasn't followed through on one yet. The fact that she saved it is a positive signal.

**Q: Is this solving a real problem for you?**

> "I think it could if the app gains traction and more users. It's hard to make new friends after freshman year without it being weird. You can't just walk up to someone and say hey let's hang out. But if you're both in a pod for the same thing it gives you a reason to connect and that takes the pressure off."

She identified that Orbit gives people a shared context to meet around, which lowers the social awkwardness of approaching strangers — a real barrier especially after the first year when everyone already has established groups.

**Q: What would bring you back tomorrow?**

> "If I got a notification that someone in my hiking pod posted a plan for this weekend, I'd definitely open the app. Right now it's quiet though. I don't want to be the first one to suggest something in a group of strangers, so someone has to break the ice."

She's willing to engage but hesitant to initiate. This points to a potential activation problem where everyone joins a pod but waits for someone else to make the first move.
