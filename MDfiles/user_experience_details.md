## Interview 1: Casey

**Background:** TA Casey Felton, PhD candidate in Linguistics. Used the app alongside Nishad. Adrian conducted this interview, repeating the Task, AI Vibe Check, and Debrief from the Milestone 2 interviews to measure improvement.

### Phase 2: The Task (Core Loop)

**Scenario:** "Imagine you are planning a trip for next weekend. Show me how you would use this tool to get a list of restaurants."

Casey was able to plan a trip quickly with no guidance and minimal confusion — a significant improvement over last week's version. He took some time to figure out the voyage page, but that was expected and not a blocker.

### Phase 3: AI Vibe Check

Casey found the AI suggestions interesting and thought they were reasonably accurate. He noted they were a bit generic due to limited profile data and was hesitant to fully trust them since the underlying methods were unclear. That said, he viewed them as a positive addition because they're just suggestions, not requirements.

### Qualitative Feedback

- The app isn't widely used yet, making it hard for users outside the Orbit team to find help. He suggested a support page or troubleshooting tool.
- He recommended breaking sports into subcategories as the app scales, though the current broad grouping works fine for now.
- UI was clean. Everything attempted worked as expected — no bugs encountered.
- Casey does not struggle with making friends and has solid social groups. He tried Orbit to find new events outside his usual activities.
- He hypothesizes it could solve that problem but can't confirm without a real community.
- He did not use the flex mission feature — he prefers structured events due to his busy PhD schedule. His last spontaneous hangout was a month ago with existing friends.
- He believes the app may solve a real problem for people who struggle to meet others. The pod system could serve as a good icebreaker.
- He acknowledged he may not be the target audience given his solid social life and tight schedule.

### Post-Usage Script

**Pain (Push):** Casey's last attempt to find friends for activities was through Discord — joining a server from Reddit, talking to people, and planning a hangout. The worst part was coordination: getting multiple people to agree on something took a long time.

**Promise (Pull):** He hoped to find meetup coordination tools that would prevent the indecisiveness and slowness of group planning. He said this was somewhat present but not fully developed. The app did organize groups and suggest activities, which he appreciated.

**Anxiety (Friction):** Same as AI Vibe Check above — he found suggestions interesting but generic, and was unsure of the methods behind them. Ultimately saw them as a net positive since they're non-coercive.

**Inertia (Status Quo):** He didn't abandon Orbit for the old way, but noted that Discord has vastly more users and servers without size limits, giving more options for screening people. Pods are smaller and more focused, which is good for initiating real-life meetups but limits the selection pool. He understood that Orbit's design prioritizes facilitating in-person events, a constraint Discord doesn't share.

### Quantitative Summary

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Days of usage | 1 day, ~3 hours |
| Activities | Joined a few pods, created 2 pods, made an account, explored voyage |
| Inertia | Solid friend group, too busy for spontaneous events |
| Push | Not enough people on Orbit yet |

## Interview 2: Nishad

**Background:** CS new grad. Used the app alongside Casey for 3–5 hours. Adrian conducted this interview. Multi-day testing was not feasible — rapid bug fixes were being deployed and the community-dependent app had no real user base yet.

### Qualitative Feedback

- UI was clean and simple. He believed any younger or tech-savvy person could figure it out with minimal time, though less experienced users might take longer. He doubted those users were the target audience anyway.
- AI suggestions were solid and did take interests into account. He noted that match percentages were clustered similarly across events — Adrian explained this was because the AI currently only uses tags and event descriptions, limiting differentiation. Nishad still saw it as an improvement.
- He identified a strong use case: people who have friends but have interests those friends don't share. Nishad is into boxing and jiu jitsu, but his friends aren't. He wouldn't use Orbit for regular hangouts but would use it to find people for niche hobbies.
- He raised the **cold start problem** — the app's value depends on having users, but it's hard to attract users when the value isn't there yet. This creates a catch-22 for social platforms.
- He suggested focusing on a single niche (e.g., a school club) and building a cult following there before expanding to other niches.

### Post-Usage Script

**Pain (Push):** His last attempt was trying to find martial arts partners by approaching people on the heavy bags at the gym. It worked but was awkward.

**Promise (Pull):** He hoped the app would suggest people to him and break the ice, skipping the awkward stages. He said the pod system does this well by placing users into groups. Without a real community he couldn't fully evaluate it, but he sees the vision — pods help shy people form groups.

**Anxiety (Friction):** He wasn't worried about the AI. Best case, suggestions improve the experience. Worst case, he just browses manually. He isn't fully sold on AI but sees it as risk-free.

**Inertia (Status Quo):** The old way (cold approaches) is better for developing social skills and gives immediate yes/no answers. But Orbit is better for planning real events — you wouldn't cold-approach a stranger to invite them to a weekend BBQ. There's no good way to find people for planned group activities unless they're already your friends. Orbit fills that gap.

## Interview 4: Audrey

**Background:** College student looking to expand her social circle beyond her major.

### Q&A

**What problem were you trying to solve?**
Most of her friends are from her major and they always do the same things. She wants to branch out and meet people she wouldn't normally cross paths with — interested in hiking, pottery, and live music — but doesn't know where to start. She's not looking to replace her friend group, just expand it in a low-pressure way.

**First impressions after using it today?**
It felt welcoming and casual — not a dating app or LinkedIn. She signed up and joined a weekend hiking pod and a thrift shopping pod quickly. The casual framing made her comfortable putting herself out there, which is notable since comfort level is likely a bigger barrier than usability for many users.

**What did you think of the AI suggestions?**
Some were great — it suggested a live music pod she loved. But it also recommended a volleyball league, which was completely off. She wanted a better onboarding questionnaire to help the AI nail her preferences. This could be a quick win for recommendation quality.

**Did you try the flex mission feature?**
Yes — she saved one for trying a new coffee shop near campus. She liked that it gives you a reason to just go do something instead of debating in the group chat. She hasn't followed through yet but is more open to flex missions than most other testers.

**Is this solving a real problem for you?**
She thinks it could if the app gains traction. Making new friends after freshman year is hard — you can't just walk up to someone and suggest hanging out. Being in a pod together gives a shared context and takes the pressure off. The pod structure acts as a natural icebreaker.

**What would bring you back tomorrow?**
A notification that someone in her hiking pod posted a weekend plan. She doesn't want to be the first to suggest something in a group of strangers — someone has to break the ice. This points to an activation problem where everyone joins but waits for others to move first.

### Quantitative Summary

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Days of usage | 1 |
| Total logins | 2 |
| Pods joined | 3 |
| Flex missions | 1 saved, not completed |
| Retention signal | Positive but needs pod activity to sustain |

### Takeaways

She represents a strong use case — someone who wants to expand socially but lacks a natural entry point. The casual tone resonated and she engaged more broadly than other testers. The main risk is the cold start issue: pods feel empty and no one wants to go first. Auto-suggested weekend plans or icebreaker prompts could help users like her.

**Pain:** Social circle stuck within her major — same people, same activities.
**Promise:** Low-pressure way to meet new people through shared interests without it feeling forced.
**Inertia:** She has friends already, so there's no urgent reason to change.
**Push:** Pods are quiet and no one wants to go first, so nothing pulls her back.
